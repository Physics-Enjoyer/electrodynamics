Section 1.1.1 Vector Operations#

Vectors are mathematical objects that follow certain properties. In physics vectors are generally described as something that has both a magnitude and a direction.

Examples of vectors are:

  • Displacement (not distance)

  • Velocity

  • Acceleration

  • Force

  • Momentum

Objects that are just a numerical value and do not have a direction associated with them are called scalers.

Examples of scalers are:

  • Mass

  • Charge

  • Density

  • Temperature

Vectors can be notated by either using boldface letters (e.g. \(\bf{r}\), \(\bf{v}\), \(\bf{a}\), \(\bf{F}\), \(\bf{p}\)) or placing an arrow over the letter (e.g. \(\vec{r}\), \(\vec{v}\), \(\vec{a}\), \(\vec{F}\), \(\vec{p}\)).

The magnitude of a vector can be notated either by putting vertical bars around it such as |a| or simply using non-boldface letters \(a\).

A vector that has the same magnitude of \(\bf{a}\) but points in the opposite direction of \(\bf{a}\) is \(-\bf{a}\)

Examples of vectors are below:

For our purposes we define four main vector operations. The four operations are:

  • Addition

  • Three types of multiplication

    • Scaler multiplication

    • The dot product (also known as the scaler product)

    • the cross product (also known as the vector product)

Addition and scaler multiplication are both relatively simple.

Vector addition#

Two vectors can be added together by adding the components of one vector to the components of another vector. But what is a vector component? Well if you have made it to these notes you should already know what a component is. It is the magnitude of the vector in a particular direction.

There are two methods for adding vectors. Graphical addition of vectors and analytical addition of vectors. I will first discuss graphical addition then move on to analytical addition which will likely be used for the remainder of the course.

Graphical Addition of Vectors#

Graphical addition of vectors is fairly simple. To add two vectors \(\bf{A}\) and \(\bf{B}\) place the tail of \(\bf{B}\) on the tip of \(\bf{A}\)

Analytical addition of vectors#

If you look in the code block above this is what I actually already did in order to plot the resultant vector C. Analytical addition is simply element wise addition. Given two vectors \(\bf{A} = (3, 4)\) and \(\bf{B} = (2, 1)\) the resultant vector \(\bf{C} = (3+2, 4+1)\)

In more general terms if \(\bf{A} = (a_1, a_2, a_3, ..., a_n)\) and \(\bf{B} = (b_1, b_2, b_3, ..., b_n)\) then \(\bf{A}+\bf{B} = \bf{C} = (a_1+b_1, a_2+b_2, a_3+b_3, ..., a_n+b_n)\)

This can be simplified with some notation shortcuts.

\[ \bf{C}_\mu = \bf{A}_\mu + \bf{B}_\mu \]

Where \(\mu\) is the index of the vector.

Properties of vector addition#

Vector addition has all the properties you would expect. It is commutative and associative. Meaning the order you add vectors doesn’t matter no matter how many vectors you have.

\[ \bf{A} + \bf{B} = \bf{B} + \bf{A} \]
\[ \bf{A} + (\bf{B} + \bf{C}) = (\bf{A} + \bf{B}) + \bf{C} = (\bf{A} + \bf{C}) + \bf{B} \]

Vector subtraction#

There’s one more thing to mention about vector addition and thats vector subtraction. Vector subtraction is defined by the addition of the additive inverse. That means that \(\bf{A} - \bf{B}\) really means \(\bf{A} + (-\bf{B})\) and \(-\bf{B} = -1\cdot\bf{B}\) or \(-1\) times each element of \(\bf{B}\) which is just multiplication by a scaler which is covered next.

Vector Multiplication#

There are 3 ways to multiply vectors

  • Multiplication by a scaler

  • Dot Product

  • Cross Product

Multiplication by a scaler is multiplying a vector by a scaler or a number. It scales the vector up or down. The other two vector multiplications, the dot product and the cross product, are ways of multiplying 2 vectors together.

Multiplication by a scaler#

Given a vector \(\bf{A}\) and a scaler \(\alpha\) then scaler multiplication is simply the scaler \(\alpha\) multiplied by each element of \(\bf{A}\)

\[ \bf{A} = (a_1, a_2, a_3, ..., a_n) \]

then

\[ \alpha\bf{A} = (\alpha a_1, \alpha a_2, \alpha a_3, ..., \alpha a_n) \]

Dot Product#

To multiply 2 vectors together with the dot product is similar to vector addition only at the end we sum all the results together.

\[ \bf{A} \cdot \bf{B} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + a_3b_3 + ..., +a_nb_n \]

The dot product returns a scaler value. In other words a number. In summation notation this is:

\[ \bf{A} \cdot \bf{B} = \sum_{\mu}a_{\mu}b_{\mu} \]

Another way of defining the dot product is by using geometry.

\[ \bf{A}\cdot\bf{B} = |A||B|\cos(\theta) \]

Where \(\theta\) is the angle between the two vectors.

Properties of the Dot Product#

The dot product is commutative and distributive.

Commutativity

\[ \bf{A}\cdot\bf{B} = \bf{B}\cdot\bf{A} \]

Distributivity

\[ \bf{A} \cdot (\bf{B} + \bf{C}) = \bf{A}\cdot\bf{B} + \bf{A}\cdot\bf{C} \]

Cross Product#

The cross product of two vectors is a way to multiply two vectors and get another vector. The definition of a cross product is

\[ \bf{A}\times\bf{B} = |\bf{A}||\bf{B}|\sin(\theta)\bf{\hat{n}} \]

Where \(\bf{\hat{n}}\) is a unit vector perpendicular to the plane made by \(\bf{A}\) and \(\bf{B}\)

The direction of the cross product can be found by using the right hand rule.

Properties of the cross product#

The cross product is distributive

\[ \bf{A}\times(\bf{B} + \bf{C}) = \bf{A}\times\bf{B} + \bf{A}\times\bf{C} \]

but not commutative

\[ \bf{A}\times\bf{B} \neq \bf{B}\times\bf{A} \]

In fact

\[\bf{A}\times\bf{B} = -(\bf{B}\times\bf{A})\]

Geometrically the cross product is the area of the parallelogram

Show code cell source Hide code cell source 
A = np.array([1, 3])
B = np.array([3, 0])

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.arrow(0, 0, A[0], A[1], head_width=0.05, length_includes_head=True)

ax.arrow(A[0], A[1], B[0], B[1], head_width=0.05, length_includes_head=True, ls="--")
ax.arrow(B[0], B[1], A[0], A[1], head_width=0.05, length_includes_head=True, ls="--")
plt.show()

The cross product can also be defined via linear algebra as a solution to the determinant

\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\begin{split} \bf{A}\times\bf{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{\textbf{x}} & \hat{\textbf{y}} & \hat{\textbf{z}} \\ a_x & a_y & a_z \\ b_x & b_y & b_z \\ \end{vmatrix}\end{split}\\\begin{split}= \hat{x}\begin{vmatrix} a_y & a_z \\ b_y & b_z \end{vmatrix} - \hat{y}\begin{vmatrix} a_x & a_z \\ b_x & b_y \end{vmatrix} + \hat{z}\begin{vmatrix} a_x & a_y \\ b_x & b_y \end{vmatrix} \end{split}\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

where \(\hat{\textbf{x}}\), \(\hat{\textbf{y}}\), and \(\hat{\textbf{z}}\) are the unit vectors in the \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\) direction.
