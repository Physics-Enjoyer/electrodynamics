Vector and Scaler Quantities#

A scaler is a mathematical object that has only a magnitude. In other words it is just a number

Examples of scalers

  • Temperature

  • Energy

  • Quantity of something

  • Speed

A vector on the other hand is a mathematical object that has a magnitude and a direction.

Examples of vectors

  • Velocity (not speed)

  • Momentum

  • Angular Momentum

  • Acceleration

  • Position

There are many more examples of both vectors and scalers but thats enough for now.

A unit vector is a vector with a magnitude of \(1\). Unit vectors are dimensionless and are calculated by dividing the vector by the magnitude of the vector.

\[\hat{A} = \frac{\vec{A}}{|\vec{A}|}\]

Vector Addition#

Adding vectors can be accomplished a few different ways. Graphical or analytical.

Graphical addition#

Given two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) shown below:

Properties of vector addition#

Vector addition is

  • Associative

    • \(\vec{A} + \vec{B} + \vec{C} = (\vec{A}+\vec{B})+\vec{C} = \vec{A}+(\vec{B}+\vec{C})\)

  • Commutative

    • \(\vec{A}+\vec{B} = \vec{B} + \vec{A}\)

Scaler Multiplication#

A vector can be multiplied by a scaler

\(k\vec{A} = k(A\hat{A}) = (kA)\hat{A}\)

Where \(k\) is some scaler quantity, \(A\) is the magnitude of the vector \(\vec{A}\) and \(\hat{A}\) is the unit vector pointing in the direction of \(\vec{A}\). Note that \(k\) only changes the magnitude and not the direction except in the case of a negative scaler than it reverses the direction.

Dot product of two vectors#

Two vectors can be multiplied together via the Dot Product.

The dot product is defined as \(\vec{A}\cdot\vec{B} = AB\cos(\theta)\) where \(\theta\) is the angle between the two vectors and \(AB\) is \(|\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\) the product of the magnitude of each vector.

A = np.array([5, 0])
B = np.array([2, 2])
r = 0.5
theta = np.linspace(0, np.arctan(1))
x = r*np.cos(theta)
y = r*np.sin(theta)
fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.arrow(0, 0, A[0], A[1], head_width=0.1)
ax.annotate("$\\vec{A}$", (1,1.2))
ax.arrow(0, 0, B[0], B[1], head_width=0.1)
ax.annotate("$\\vec{B}$", (2.5, 0.1))
ax.arrow(0, 0, 2, 0, head_width=0.1, color="red")
ax.annotate("$A\\cos(\\theta)$", (1, 0.1))
ax.plot(x,y)
ax.annotate("$\\theta$", (0.6, 0.25))
ax.vlines(2, 0, 2, linestyles=":")
ax.set_aspect("equal")
ax.set_title("Dot Prodcut of 2 vectors")
plt.show()

Properties of the dot product#

  • The dot product of two perpendicular vectors is always zero

  • If the angle between the two vectors is greater than \(90^{\circ}\) then the dot product is negative

  • Dot products are commutative

    • \(\vec{A}\cdot\vec{B} = \vec{B}\cdot\vec{A}\)

  • Dot products are distributive

    • \(\vec{A}\cdot(\vec{B} + \vec{C}) = \vec{A}\cdot\vec{B} + \vec{A}\cdot\vec{C}\)

Cross product of two vectors#

The cross product is defined as

\[\vec{A}\times\vec{B} = AB\sin(\theta)\hat{n}\]

here \(AB\) is the same as the dot product, i.e. the product of the magnitudes of each of the vectors but instead of multiplying by \(\cos{\theta}\) its multiplied by \(\sin(\theta)\). The \(\hat{n}\) is the unit vector that is perpendicular to the plane made by the two vectors and can be found using the right hand rule that we learned about in earlier physics classes.

The magnitude of the cross product is the area of the parallelogram formed by the two vectors as shown below.

Show code cell source Hide code cell source 
A = np.array([1, 2])
B = np.array([4, 0])

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.arrow(0, 0, A[0], A[1], head_width=0.1, color="red")
ax.arrow(0, 0, B[0], B[1], head_width=0.1, color="blue")
ax.arrow(A[0], A[1], B[0], B[1], head_width=0.1, color="blue", ls="--")
ax.arrow(B[0], B[1], A[0], A[1], head_width=0.1, color="red", ls="--")
ax.annotate("$\\vec{A}$", (0.25, 1))
ax.annotate("$\\vec{B}$", (2, 0.1))
ax.set_aspect("equal")
ax.set_title("Cross Product of $\\vec{A}$ and $\\vec{B}$")
plt.show()

Properties of the cross product#

  • The cross product is not commutative and is in fact anti-commutative

    • \(\vec{A}\times\vec{B} \neq \vec{B}\times\vec{A}\)

    • \(\vec{A}\times\vec{B} = -\vec{B}\times\vec{A}\)

  • The cross product is distributive

    • \(\vec{A}\times(\vec{B} + \vec{C}) = \vec{A}\times\vec{B} + \vec{A}\times\vec{C}\)

Vector componenets#

Any vector can be written as a sum of other vectors. Put in another way any vector can be written as a linear combination of other vectors. For example given a vector \(\vec{D} = (3, 2)\) this can be written as a linear combination of \(\vec{A} = (0, 2)\) and \(\vec{B} = (3, 0)\). However it can also be written as a combination of the vectors \(\vec{A} = (-1, 2)\) and \(\vec{B} = (4, 0)\) or as \(\vec{A} = (3, 2)\) and \(\vec{B} = (1, -1)\)

These three examples are shown below:

Note that this is also valid for in 3D space as well.

The magnitude of a vector is given by the generalized Pythagorean Theorem. \(|\vec{D}| = \sqrt{A^2+B^2}\) or in 3D space \(|\vec{D}| = \sqrt{A^2+B^2+C^2}\)

Vectors can be written in component form such as \(\vec{A} = A_x\hat{x}+A_y\hat{y}+A_z\hat{z}\) where \(\hat{x}\), \(\hat{y}\), and \(\hat{z}\) are the unit vectors along the \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\) axes.

With components many of the operations described above become much easier.

Vector addition becomes:

\(\vec{A}+\vec{B} = (A_x+B_x)\hat{x} + (A_y+B_y)\hat{y} + (A_z+B_z)\hat{z}\)

The dot product becomes

\(\vec{A}\cdot\vec{B} = A_xB_x + A_yB_y + A_zB_z\)

This can be explained by:

\[ \vec{A}\cdot\vec{B} = (A_x\hat{x} + A_y\hat{y} + A_z\hat{z})(B_x\hat{x} + B_y\hat{y} + B_z\hat{z}) \]
\[\begin{split} = (A_xB_x)\hat{x}\cdot\hat{x} + (A_xB_y)\hat{x}\cdot\hat{y} +(A_xB_z)\hat{x}\cdot\hat{y} \\ + (A_yB_x)\hat{y}\cdot\hat{x} + (A_yB_y)\hat{y}\cdot\hat{y} + (A_yB_z)\hat{y}\cdot\hat{z} \\ + (A_zB_x)\hat{z}\cdot\hat{x} + (A_zB_y)\hat{z}\cdot\hat{y} + (A_zB_z)\hat{z}\cdot\hat{z} \end{split}\]

But we know that \(\hat{x}\cdot\hat{y} = \hat{x}\cdot\hat{z} = \hat{y}\cdot\hat{z} = 0\) so the only terms that remain are the terms with \(\hat{x}\cdot\hat{x} = \hat{y}\cdot\hat{y} = \hat{z}\cdot\hat{z} = 1\) and the final result is:

\(\vec{A}\cdot\vec{B} = A_xB_x + A_yB_y + A_zB_z\)

We can express this much more compactly using summation notation.

\[ \vec{A}\cdot\vec{B} = \sum_{i=1}^3A_iB_i \]
